# AI Writing Pattern Catalog

**This file is a reference catalog, not an instruction set.** When this skill is loaded, use the positive routing constraints in SKILL.md to guide generation. Consult this catalog only as a post-generation diagnostic — scan the output for these patterns after writing, not during. The routing constraints make most of these patterns structurally unlikely; this catalog catches what slips through.

Source: synthesized from tropes.fyi, Wikipedia's WikiProject AI Cleanup, stop-slop, and deslop.

## Word-Level Tells

### Overused AI Vocabulary
delve, tapestry, vibrant, pivotal, crucial, vital, landscape, navigate, unpack, foster, leverage, multifaceted, nuanced, interplay, holistic, robust, seamless, cutting-edge, groundbreaking, nestled

### Copula Avoidance
AI avoids "is/are/has" because repetition penalties push it toward fancier constructions:
- "serves as" instead of "is"
- "stands as" instead of "is"
- "represents" instead of "is"
- "features" instead of "has"
- "boasts" instead of "has"

### Significance Inflation
pivotal, crucial, vital, testament, underscores, highlights, showcases, game-changing, transformative, paradigm-shifting, unprecedented

### Intensifier Accumulation
really, just, literally, genuinely, honestly, simply, actually, deeply, truly, fundamentally, inherently, inevitably, incredibly, extremely, remarkably

### Promotional Adjectives
vibrant, stunning, world-class, must-visit, breathtaking, rich cultural heritage, enduring legacy, captivating, awe-inspiring

## Sentence-Level Tells

### Negative Parallelism (the single most identified AI tell)
- "It's not X — it's Y"
- "Not because X. Because Y."
- "Not only X, but also Y"
- "This isn't about X — it's about Y"
- "Rather than X, focus on Y"

### Self-Posed Rhetorical Questions
- "The X? A Y." (question immediately answered)
- "So what does this mean? It means..."
- "But why does this matter? Because..."

### Throat-Clearing Openers
- "Here's the thing:"
- "The uncomfortable truth is"
- "Let me be clear:"
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "In the realm of"
- "It turns out"

### Emphasis Crutches
- "Full stop."
- "That's it. That's the [noun]."
- "Let that sink in."
- "And that's okay."
- "The best part?"
- "And here's the kicker"

### Superficial -ing Analyses
- "highlighting the importance of..."
- "demonstrating the need for..."
- "showcasing the potential of..."
- "underscoring the significance of..."

These add no information. Replace with the specific outcome or finding.

## Paragraph-Level Tells

### Structural Monotony
Every paragraph follows: topic sentence, three supporting points of equal weight, summary sentence. Repeat.

### Dramatic Fragmentation
One-sentence paragraphs for manufactured emphasis. Short punchy fragments at the end of every section. "X changed everything."

### Listicle in a Trench Coat
A bulleted list reformatted as paragraphs. Each paragraph is one point, same length, same structure, could be reordered without loss.

### Fractal Summaries
The introduction summarizes the paper. Each section opening summarizes the section. Each paragraph opening summarizes the paragraph. The conclusion summarizes the paper again.

## Composition-Level Tells

### False Ranges
"From X to Y" when no real spectrum exists. "From policy implications to ethical considerations" — what's in between?

### Elegant Variation (Synonym Cycling)
Using "framework," then "model," then "paradigm," then "approach" for the same concept to avoid repetition. Consistency is not repetition.

### Historical Analogy Stacking
"Just as the printing press transformed..." "Much like the Industrial Revolution..." — using historical analogies as substitutes for analysis.

### Rule of Three Overuse
Everything comes in threes: three benefits, three challenges, three implications. Real analysis doesn't organize itself this neatly.

### "Despite Its Challenges" Conclusion
"Despite its challenges, X remains a Y that Z." This is the most common AI conclusion pattern. Every challenge is acknowledged but immediately dismissed.

### Formulaic Future Sections
"Future research should explore..." "As the field continues to evolve..." "Only time will tell..." These add nothing. End with your strongest finding or its most important implication.

## Academic-Specific Tells

### The Student-Teacher Voice
Explaining things the reader already knows. Defining basic terms for an expert audience. Providing textbook-level background in a research paper.

### Performative Limitations
"This study is not without limitations" followed by a list of limitations that don't actually threaten the conclusions. Real limitations acknowledge genuine weaknesses.

### Vague Attribution
"Experts say," "observers note," "research has shown," "the literature suggests" — without naming anyone or any study.

### Citation as Decoration
Parenthetical citations sprinkled at the end of every sentence without narrative integration. Citations should serve the argument, not decorate it.

## How to Use This Catalog

After generating text, scan the output against these patterns. If any appear, the diagnosis is usually a weak derivation — strengthen the register target and texture vocabulary rather than patching individual sentences. During generation, rely on the routing constraints in SKILL.md, not this list.
