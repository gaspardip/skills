# Discipline Registers

Academic writing conventions vary by field. Choosing the wrong register is itself an AI tell — models default to a generic "academic" voice that belongs to no actual discipline.

## STEM (Sciences, Technology, Engineering, Mathematics)

**Voice:** concise, method-focused, compressed. Every sentence earns its place with information.

**Conventions:**
- Passive voice acceptable in methods sections ("Samples were collected") but active voice for argumentation ("We propose", "Our results demonstrate")
- Numbered hypotheses (H1, H2) when testing multiple predictions
- Statistical reporting follows APA or field-specific standards: F(2,47) = 4.12, p = .02, d = 0.61
- Effect sizes alongside p-values — p-values alone are insufficient
- Equations introduced before use, variables defined at first occurrence
- Figures and tables referenced in-text before they appear

**Register targets:** Nature methods sections, IEEE transactions, PLOS ONE, Journal of the American Chemical Society

**Sentence characteristics:**
- Short to medium (12-25 words average)
- SVO structure (subject-verb-object)
- Quantities over adjectives ("reduced latency by 40ms" not "significantly improved performance")
- If/then over "might" ("If the sample exceeds 500, stratified sampling is required")

## Social Sciences

**Voice:** balanced active/passive, theory-driven, variable-focused. Longer paragraphs than STEM, shorter than humanities.

**Conventions:**
- First person plural standard ("We argue", "Our analysis reveals")
- Theoretical framing expected in introductions — name the theory, the gap, the contribution
- APA citation style with author-date
- Construct operationalization must be explicit (how was "social capital" measured?)
- Limitations section is mandatory and should be specific, not performative
- Effect sizes reported alongside significance tests

**Register targets:** American Sociological Review, Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, Comparative Political Studies, Administrative Science Quarterly

**Sentence characteristics:**
- Medium (18-30 words average)
- Theory terms woven into empirical claims ("Bourdieu's concept of cultural capital predicts that...")
- Hedging calibrated to evidence strength: "suggests" for one study, "demonstrates" for meta-analyses
- Parenthetical citations clustered for synthesis: (Lee, 2019; Nakamura, 2020; dos Santos, 2021)

## Humanities

**Voice:** first person singular, interpretive, argumentative. Longer analytical paragraphs. The writer's intellectual presence is expected.

**Conventions:**
- First person singular common ("I argue", "I read this passage as")
- Close reading and interpretive argument valued over empirical breadth
- Direct engagement with other scholars' positions ("Spivak's reading overlooks...")
- Block quotations for passages under direct analysis
- Footnotes for tangential but relevant elaboration
- Chicago or MLA citation style

**Register targets:** Critical Inquiry, PMLA, The Journal of Philosophy, Past & Present, Representations

**Sentence characteristics:**
- Long (25-40 words average), with subordinate clauses
- Concession-then-assertion structure common ("While Derrida's critique destabilizes the binary, it does not follow that...")
- Abstract nouns grounded by specific textual references
- Qualifications are part of the argument, not hedging

## Interdisciplinary

**Voice:** active voice default, bridge-building, term-defining. Must be readable by scholars from multiple fields.

**Conventions:**
- First person plural standard
- Define terms from each contributing field at first use — never assume shared vocabulary
- Bridge jargon explicitly: "What economists call 'externalities' — costs imposed on third parties not involved in the transaction —"
- Methodological pluralism acknowledged, not defended
- Literature review organized by theme across disciplines, not by discipline

**Register targets:** Science, Nature Human Behaviour, Annual Review of [interdisciplinary field], PNAS

**Sentence characteristics:**
- Medium (15-28 words average)
- Plain language with technical precision: accessible but not dumbed down
- Analogies welcome when crossing disciplines ("The immune system's memory cells function analogously to...")
- Signal which discipline a claim comes from: "Evolutionary biologists have shown...", "In political economy, this is known as..."

## Business / Management

**Voice:** practitioner-aware, evidence-based but accessible. Straddles academic rigor and professional readability.

**Conventions:**
- First person plural for the research team
- Managerial implications section expected
- Real company names and cases ground theoretical claims
- APA citation style
- Tables summarize variables, measures, and results compactly

**Register targets:** Harvard Business Review research articles, Academy of Management Journal, Strategic Management Journal, Organization Science

**Sentence characteristics:**
- Medium (16-26 words average)
- Construct-then-example pattern ("Dynamic capabilities — a firm's ability to reconfigure resources in response to environmental shifts — explain why Nokia failed to adapt to smartphones while Apple thrived")
- Active voice strongly preferred
- Numbers and percentages used liberally
