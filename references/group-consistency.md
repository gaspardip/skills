# Group Project Voice Unification

Group projects fail detection not because individual sections sound AI-generated, but because sections sound like they were written by different AIs — or by humans who made no effort to sound like the same team.

## The Problem

Each team member writes their section independently. The result:
- Section A uses "this paper argues" while Section B uses "we contend" while Section C uses "it can be argued"
- Section A hedges everything, Section B is assertive, Section C is somewhere in between
- Transition vocabulary shifts between sections
- Paragraph length and complexity vary wildly at section boundaries
- The introduction promises things the conclusion doesn't deliver (different authors)

A professor reads 25 of these. The inconsistency is obvious.

## The Protocol

### Step 1: Shared Derivation

The group agrees on ONE derivation before anyone writes anything:

- **Reader:** who is grading this? What do they care about?
- **Register:** pick ONE publication or author whose tone to match across all sections
- **Texture:** agree on 10-15 domain terms everyone will use
- **Pronoun convention:** pick one and stick to it ("we argue" for the whole paper)
- **Hedging level:** agree on a scale (e.g., "confident with evidence, cautious without")

Write this derivation down. Every team member gets it before writing.

### Step 2: Voice Sample Anchor

Pick the strongest writer in the group. Have them write the introduction (or one strong paragraph). This becomes the voice anchor — the reference sample that all other sections match.

Extract from this anchor:
- Average sentence length
- Paragraph length range
- Transition style (explicit? implicit? mixed?)
- Hedging patterns (how they qualify claims)
- How they introduce citations (narrative? parenthetical?)
- How they open paragraphs (topic sentence? question? data?)

### Step 3: Section Integration Checklist

Before combining sections, check each pair of adjacent sections:

- [ ] **Pronoun consistency:** same first-person convention throughout
- [ ] **Hedging calibration:** similar certainty levels for similar evidence strengths
- [ ] **Paragraph length:** no section averages more than 30% longer or shorter than the anchor
- [ ] **Sentence length distribution:** no section has notably different short/medium/long ratios
- [ ] **Transition style:** consistent connectors at section boundaries
- [ ] **Terminology:** same term for the same concept throughout (not "framework" in one section and "model" in another for the same thing)
- [ ] **Citation style:** all sections use the same format and integration pattern
- [ ] **Tense consistency:** same tense for the same type of claim (present for established theory, past for specific study results)
- [ ] **Paragraph opening patterns:** no section exclusively uses topic sentences while another exclusively uses questions

### Step 4: Seam Smoothing

Section boundaries are the most detectable seams. For each boundary:

1. Read the last paragraph of Section A and the first paragraph of Section B as one unit
2. Does the tone shift? Fix it.
3. Does the vocabulary shift? Align the domain terms.
4. Is there a logical bridge? The last sentence of A should create an expectation that B's first sentence fulfills.
5. Does the level of abstraction shift? If A ends with a specific case and B opens with a broad claim, add a transitional sentence that moves from specific to general.

### Step 5: Introduction-Conclusion Alignment

These two sections must be written (or at least reviewed) by the same person or in the same session:

- Every claim in the conclusion must appear in the body
- The introduction's thesis should be echoed (not repeated verbatim) in the conclusion
- The introduction's scope should match the conclusion's scope — don't promise what wasn't delivered
- The tone of these two sections anchors the entire paper

## Common Group Project Failures

| Problem | What the professor sees | Fix |
|---------|------------------------|-----|
| Voice shift at section boundaries | "This reads like three different papers stapled together" | Shared derivation + seam smoothing |
| Introduction doesn't match conclusion | "The paper promises X but delivers Y" | Same author writes or reviews both |
| Inconsistent terminology | "Is 'framework' and 'model' the same thing here?" | Shared texture vocabulary list |
| Uneven quality | "Section 3 is clearly stronger than sections 1-2" | Voice anchor + integration checklist |
| Excessive hedging in some sections | "The author of section 2 seems unsure of everything" | Agreed hedging calibration |
